The problem

Why we built this

Every knowledge worker I know uses ChatGPT for the easy things. The blog outline. The generic email. The brainstorm. But when the document has a client's name, a salary, a legal detail, an NDA — they close the tab. They go back to doing it by hand. Or worse, they paste it anyway and hope nobody notices.

That's huge. It's the half of their work that actually pays them, and they can't use AI for it.

The problem

Cloud AI needs your data

Every prompt, every uploaded file, every piece of context you add — all of it leaves your machine. It goes to a data center you don't control. The vendor's privacy policy may say "we won't train on your data," but you're trusting, not verifying. And the NDA you signed says "don't share this with third parties."

The fix

Run the AI on your laptop

The model is a file on your disk. The runtime runs it on your CPU/GPU. Nothing touches the network. Turn off WiFi and it still works. Your data isn't "private by policy", it's private by physics.

The stack

Three pieces. That's it.

Despite how advanced it looks, this system is three independent tools layered together. Each one is open source, well-tested, and does one thing.

🧠
The brain

Qwen 2.5 (7B)

The AI model. 7 billion parameters trained by Alibaba. 4.7 GB on disk. Released with open weights (free to run, modify, redistribute). Competitive with GPT-3.5 on most tasks, GPT-4-class on code and summarization.

⚙️
The runtime

Ollama

The engine that loads the model into memory and runs it when you ask. Exposes a local HTTP API at localhost:11434. Open source (MIT). Handles everything: model downloads, memory management, streaming, GPU acceleration.

🖼️
The interface

Purely Personal

The desktop app. Built with Electron. Talks to Ollama over the local loopback. Adds chat, drag-and-drop, saved prompts, conversation history, templates, folder indexing (RAG), and dark mode. This is what you use.

These three pieces have nothing to do with each other. If Ollama disappears tomorrow, qwen2.5 still runs on any other runtime. If Qwen is replaced next month by something better, Ollama pulls it instantly. If Purely Personal breaks, any other local-AI frontend (Open WebUI, LM Studio, even curl) still works.

The streaming flow, step by step

You type a prompt and hit Enter

The Electron app captures your message, builds a JSON payload with the conversation history, model name, and streaming flag.

The app POSTs to http://localhost:11434/api/chat

A plain HTTP request over the loopback interface. No DNS lookup. No TLS handshake with a remote server. It never leaves the machine.

Ollama loads the model into RAM (first time only)

On the first message after launch, Ollama loads 4.7 GB of weights into memory. 15–30 seconds. After that, it stays in RAM and responds in milliseconds.

Qwen 2.5 predicts the next token

The model takes your context, runs a forward pass, and returns the next likely token. Then the next. Then the next. Hundreds of times a second on Apple Silicon.

Tokens stream back as Server-Sent Events

The response comes back as a chunked JSON stream. The app reads each chunk as it arrives and appends to the chat bubble in real time.

Stream ends. Markdown is rendered. Done.

When the stream completes, the app runs a markdown pass on the full reply (headings, code blocks, bold, lists), strips any em dashes (AI-tell), and stores the conversation to localStorage.

The brain

What is Qwen 2.5?

The model that does the thinking. Released by Alibaba's Qwen team in September 2024. Completely open-weights, meaning you can download it, run it, modify it, ship it in your product, without asking anyone.

Why Qwen 2.5 specifically?

Three reasons:

  • Ceiling. It's the best open-weights model in its size class as of early 2026. Beats Llama 3.1, Mistral 7B, and Phi 3 on most benchmarks. Especially strong on code, math, and summarization.
  • Size fit. 7B parameters quantized to 4-bit = 4.7 GB on disk, ~5 GB in RAM. Fits on an 8 GB laptop. A bigger model (14B, 70B) would need more RAM than most students have.
  • License. Released under Qwen's own permissive license. You can use it commercially, modify it, redistribute it, as long as you don't use it to harm people or launder data.
What are "7 billion parameters"?

The model is a neural network. Every connection between two neurons has a "weight", a number that says "how much does this input matter." Qwen 2.5 (7B) has ~7,000,000,000 of those numbers.

During training, the weights are adjusted by reading trillions of words of text and nudging them so the model predicts the next word better. After training, you freeze the weights and ship them as a file.

When you chat with it, you're running math against those frozen weights. The laptop does billions of multiplications per token. Your M-series chip has specialized matrix hardware (the Neural Engine + AMX) that makes this fast.

What's inside the 4.7 GB file on your disk?

Pure numbers. The model file (.gguf format) is:

  • Quantized weights (~4.3 GB)
  • Model metadata (architecture, tokenizer, hyperparameters)
  • A vocabulary table (~150k tokens)

No executable code. No telemetry. No callbacks. If you hex-dump the file you'll see matrices of tiny 4-bit integers. That's the whole "brain."

What is "quantization" and why 4-bit?

The original Qwen 2.5 weights are 16-bit floating-point numbers. At 7 billion weights × 2 bytes each, that's 14 GB. Too big for a laptop.

Quantization compresses each weight to fewer bits. 4-bit quantization shrinks the file to ~4.7 GB at a ~1% quality loss. You get 3× less disk, 3× less RAM, 3× faster inference, for a barely-noticeable drop in output quality.

The trick is in how you compress: naive rounding wrecks the model. Ollama uses formats like Q4_K_M that preserve the important weight ranges while dropping the less important ones.

How does it compare to ChatGPT and Claude?

Honest answer:

  • Summarization, rewriting, explaining: Comparable. You won't notice the difference on most tasks.
  • Code review (under 5k lines): Comparable to GPT-4 Turbo on real-world bugs.
  • Hard reasoning (multi-step math, logic puzzles): Meaningfully behind. ChatGPT and Claude are ~2 model generations ahead.
  • Long context (100k+ tokens): Falls off. Qwen 2.5 (7B) has a 32k context. Claude has 200k.
  • Latest news / recent events: Training cutoff was mid-2024. Anything after that, it doesn't know.

Rule of thumb: use this for routine knowledge work on confidential data. Use the cloud for hard reasoning, long docs, and current events.

Can I swap to a different model?

Yes. Any model Ollama supports, you can pull and use:

ollama pull llama3.1:8b
ollama pull mistral:7b
ollama pull phi3:medium
ollama pull gemma2:9b
ollama pull codellama:13b

Once pulled, it shows up in the model dropdown in the top-right of the app. Switch any time. The app doesn't care which model you use; it talks to whatever Ollama has loaded.

The runtime

What is Ollama?

The engine. Think of it as a local-AI Docker: a single binary that pulls models, loads them into memory, runs inference, and exposes a clean HTTP API. Ollama is what turns "a 4.7 GB file" into "a thing you can chat with."

What does Ollama actually do?

Five things:

  • Pull. ollama pull qwen2.5:7b downloads the model from a registry.
  • Load. On first use, loads weights into RAM (or VRAM if you have a GPU).
  • Serve. Exposes HTTP endpoints at localhost:11434: /api/chat, /api/embeddings, /api/tags, etc.
  • Stream. Returns tokens as they're generated, not all at once at the end.
  • Accelerate. On Apple Silicon, uses Metal. On Nvidia/AMD GPUs, uses CUDA/ROCm. On CPU, falls back to AVX/NEON.
Why HTTP on a local port?

Because it makes everything composable. Any app, any language, any framework can talk to Ollama by sending an HTTP POST. No SDK required. No bindings to compile. You can test it with curl:

curl http://localhost:11434/api/chat -d '{
  "model": "qwen2.5:7b",
  "messages": [{"role": "user", "content": "Hi"}],
  "stream": false
}'

It also means the UI (Electron) and the runtime (Ollama) are totally decoupled. You can use Purely Personal with one model today and a different one tomorrow, with no code changes.

Does Ollama phone home?

Only once, ever: the first time you ollama pull <model>, it contacts the Ollama registry to fetch the weights. After that, zero outbound connections. No telemetry. No usage pings. No update checks unless you run ollama pull again.

Verify it yourself: on Mac, lsof -i -n -P | grep ollama. On Windows, Resource Monitor → Network tab. On Linux, ss -tupn | grep ollama. You'll see no outbound connections while you're chatting.

How fast is it?

Depends on your laptop. Rough numbers for Qwen 2.5 (7B) streaming:

  • M1 / M2 / M3 / M4 Mac (8+ GB): 40–80 tokens/sec. Feels as fast as ChatGPT.
  • Windows with modern Nvidia GPU: 60–120 tokens/sec on a 3060+. Faster than ChatGPT.
  • Windows CPU-only: 8–20 tokens/sec. Still usable for short answers.
  • Linux with Nvidia/AMD GPU: Same as Windows GPU numbers.

First-message latency is 15–30 sec while the model loads. After that, warm responses are instant.

Folder indexing

How RAG works, plainly

The "Index a folder" feature is RAG: retrieval-augmented generation. Fancy word for: chop your notes into chunks, find the relevant ones, hand them to the model. Here's how it actually works under the hood.

You pick a folder

The Electron app opens a native folder picker. The main process walks every text-readable file under it (up to 250 files, 4 MB total, 300 KB per file). Binaries are skipped.

Each file is chunked

The app breaks each file into ~900-character chunks on paragraph boundaries. Big paragraphs are split on sentence boundaries. Each chunk keeps its source file path for later citation.

Every chunk is embedded

The app calls ollama pull nomic-embed-text (274 MB, one-time). Then for every chunk, it POSTs to /api/embeddings and gets back a 768-number vector. This vector is a mathematical fingerprint of what the chunk is "about."

Everything saves to localStorage

The chunks + vectors + filenames go into localStorage under pp-rag. Not a database, just a JSON blob. Small enough to fit for up to ~500 chunks per folder.

You ask a question

When you click "Ask this", your question gets embedded the same way. The app computes cosine similarity between your question's vector and every chunk's vector. The top 5 winners are the most relevant chunks.

The model sees only those 5 chunks

The app prepends the 5 chunks to your question as "context." Qwen 2.5 reads the context, answers your question, and the app shows the answer + citations ("from notes/q3-plan.md").

Nothing indexed leaves your laptop

The chunks, the vectors, the queries, the answers — all of it is local. The embedding model runs in Ollama. The search runs in your browser. No cloud vector DB. No Pinecone. No OpenAI embeddings API. Nothing.

What's an embedding, really?

A list of numbers that represents "what a piece of text means." In Purely Personal, each chunk becomes a list of 768 numbers (a 768-dimensional vector).

The magic: pieces of text with similar meaning end up with similar vectors. "My quarterly plan" and "Q3 strategy doc" land near each other in vector space. "Cat food" lands far away.

To find the "most relevant" chunk for your question, you embed the question and then ask: which stored chunk-vector is the most similar? That's cosine similarity: a single number from -1 (opposite) to 1 (identical).

Why nomic-embed-text and not OpenAI's embeddings?

Because OpenAI's embeddings require an API call. You'd be sending every chunk of your folder to a cloud service. That breaks the whole privacy story.

Nomic-embed-text is a small embedding model (274 MB) that runs locally through Ollama. Same idea, all on your laptop. It's not quite as good as OpenAI's model, but it's good enough that you won't notice the difference on real-world queries.

The offline truth

Why it actually works without internet

A lot of "local" AI tools actually phone home. Let's be specific about how Purely Personal doesn't.

Happens locally

Everything that matters

Model inference, embeddings, your chats, your indexed folders, your saved prompts, conversation history, markdown rendering, drag-and-drop file reading. All of it. No network call at any point.

Needs internet

Two one-time things

The initial model download (ollama pull qwen2.5:7b) and app download. That's it. Once those are on your disk, unplug the ethernet, turn off WiFi, fly to Mars. Everything still works.

Prove it yourself

Three ways to verify, in increasing rigor:

  • Easy: Turn off your WiFi. Ask the AI a question. It answers.
  • Stronger: Open Activity Monitor (Mac) / Resource Monitor (Win). Filter by Purely Personal. Chat with it for 10 minutes. Bytes sent/received to external IPs: zero.
  • Paranoid: Run sudo lsof -i -n -P | grep -E "ollama|Purely" during a chat. You'll see loopback (127.0.0.1) connections only.
The privacy math

"Local" means something exact

Every cloud-AI company says they "don't train on your data." That's a policy. Policies change. Employees change. Incidents happen. With a local model, the claim isn't "we promise." The claim is "physically impossible, verify it yourself."

No prompts leave your laptop

Every byte you type stays in local memory and local disk. Never transmitted anywhere.

No files leave your laptop

Drag a contract in? It's read by the app, handed to local inference, displayed to you. Zero uploads.

No model updates without your consent

The app doesn't auto-update weights. The model you pulled in March 2026 is the same model in June 2026 unless you manually ollama pull a new version.

No telemetry

The app doesn't phone home. No crash reports. No usage pings. No anonymous "analytics." Open the source, search for "fetch(" or "http", you'll find only localhost:11434.

No vendor lock-in

Your chats are saved as plain markdown files. Your RAG index is a JSON blob in localStorage. If the app disappears tomorrow, you own all your data in open formats.

The honest list

What it can't do (yet)

No snake oil. Here's where the 7B laptop AI falls short, so you know when to reach for the cloud.

Hard multi-step reasoning

If you need the AI to chain 10 logical steps without losing the thread (complex math, multi-hop inference, deep code debugging across files), a 7B model will drift. GPT-4 and Claude 4 are meaningfully better here.

Current events

Training cutoff was mid-2024. It doesn't know what happened last month. For current events, use a cloud model with search grounding.

Very long documents

Qwen 2.5 (7B) has a 32k-token context window. That's about 120 pages. Beyond that, it starts forgetting the beginning. Claude can handle 200k. For 200-page contracts, chunk it or use cloud.

PDFs and Word docs natively

The app only reads plain-text files (.txt, .md, .csv, .json, code files). PDF and DOCX are on the v0.2 roadmap. For now: export to text first, or copy-paste.

Images, audio, video

Text-only model. Vision (llava, qwen-vl), audio (whisper), and video understanding are separate models. They exist for local, just not bundled in this app yet.

Running on ancient hardware

Needs 8+ GB of RAM. A 2014 MacBook with 4 GB is not going to work. For the super-low-spec case, there's Qwen 2.5 (3B) at 1.9 GB, but even that needs 4+ GB free RAM.

FAQ

The questions everyone asks

Is this legal to use at work?

Yes, for almost every company. The app, runtime, and model all ship with commercial-use licenses. No data leaves your machine, so there's no "sending company data to a third party" problem. That said, always check your IT policy; some regulated industries ban any AI tool until their lawyers approve it.

What happens if my laptop breaks or I reinstall?

Re-install Ollama, re-pull the model (15 min). Your chats are saved in localStorage (lost on reinstall) unless you used "Save chat" to export them as markdown. RAG indexes also live in localStorage.

Backup strategy: save important chats as markdown regularly. Those are plain files you can archive anywhere.

Can I share my indexed folder with a teammate?

Not out of the box. The index is tied to your laptop. On the v0.3 roadmap: shared team indexes over a local network.

Can I use this on a phone or tablet?

Not yet. Ollama runs on Mac/Windows/Linux. Mobile is possible (MLX on iPhone, mlc-llm on Android) but out of scope for this project.

How do I contribute or report a bug?

Open an issue or PR on GitHub: danielpaulai/Run-Worldclass-computer-locally. The codebase is small (~8k lines) and well-commented.
